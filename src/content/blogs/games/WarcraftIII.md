# Warcraft III Reforged: The Greatest Villain Origin Story in Gaming (And I Helped)

I came back to Warcraft III: Reforged expecting nostalgia. What I got was a slow-building moral crisis, an involuntary master class in RTS strategy, and the uncomfortable realization that I am, apparently, totally fine committing war crimes if the mission timer is ticking.

Let me explain.

---

## A Childhood Game I Was Too Young to Understand

I played Warcraft III as a kid but never finished it. At the time I had the attention span of a peon with a head injury, and the story went completely over my head. Coming back now, with years of StarCraft II and Dota 2 under my belt, everything clicked, and it is *so* much better than I remembered.

One of the first things that hit me was just how much DNA Warcraft III passed on to Dota 2. Characters, spells, items — they're all here in their original form, like some kind of proto-MOBA fossil record. Phantom Assassin? The Sentinel hero. Invoker? Kael'thas himself. Playing through the campaign felt like visiting the ancestral homeland of every hero I've spammed in ranked for years. Deeply humbling, and also slightly embarrassing given how long it took me to make that connection.

---

## Arthas: A Man on a Very Committed Downward Trajectory

Here's the thing about Arthas Menethil: he starts the game as a charming, capable prince who genuinely wants to protect his people. You root for him. He's a good guy!

And then, over the course of the campaign, he systematically destroys everything that made him a good guy, one catastrophically bad decision at a time.

The story of Arthas isn't a story about evil corrupting a hero. It's a story about a hero making *choices*, each one reasonable in isolation, each one a little bit worse than the last, until he's so deep in the hole that the only way out is to become the villain. It's like watching someone justify ordering dessert. *"I've already had a cheat day, so I may as well..."* Except instead of cheesecake, it's genocide.

What makes it work so brilliantly is that you're *playing* as him. Every atrocity has a loading screen and an objective marker. The game never lets you forget that **you're the one doing this**.

And the list of atrocities keeps growing. He purges his own capital. He burns his ships to strand his men and force them to keep fighting — then blames the mercenaries he hired to do it, getting his own soldiers to kill them. He takes the Lich King's cursed sword despite Muradin warning him repeatedly not to, and then proceeds to kill Muradin immediately after — a man who helped him every step of the way, who literally participated in burning those ships with him. Just disposed of. Because the sword was more important.

I was in a state of constant disbelief completing these missions. It's one thing to watch a character slowly turn evil in a film. It's another thing entirely to be the one clicking the buttons, completing the objectives, and watching the cutscene aftermath. No other medium can do that.


## The Culling of Stratholme: The Mission Where I Became a Monster

Nothing in gaming has made me feel quite like the Stratholme mission.

The setup: a city's grain supply has been tainted with a plague that turns people into undead. The population is already infected. Arthas' solution — purge the city before they turn.

Jaina and Uther refuse. They walk away. The game gives you a moment to sit with that.

And then the mission starts, and you do it anyway.

You break down doors. You kill the civilians inside. You race the clock as fast as possible to stop them from turning before they can spread.

Here's the part that haunts me: I started *optimizing*. Unit formation. Attack pathing. How to clear buildings fastest. I was genuinely focused on efficiency and somewhere around the third district I looked up from my APM spiral and thought, *what am I doing right now?*

The genius of Stratholme is that the game never calls you evil for it. Arthas believes he's right. The logic is internally consistent. And so you just... do it. Efficiently. Like a professional.

That cognitive dissonance, feeling like a competent RTS player while committing a massacre, is something I've never experienced in any other game. It doesn't lecture you. It just lets you sit in it.

That's when Arthas' descent truly begins. And you're in it with him. I can't even fault him for the decision, logically he was correct, and just like the trolley problem, he chose to save the majority. But I think what crushed him most was the reaction of his closest companions. Jaina, his lover, and Uther, his mentor, scorn him and walk away after the mission. They don't argue. They just leave. And that silence hits harder than any villain speech could.

What makes it even more ironic is who steps up to fill that void later. Anub'arak and Ner'zhul genuinely concerned for him, supportive at every turn, showing more warmth and empathy than the humans ever did. Two non-human, technically-undead figures displaying more humanity than the people who were supposed to love him. 

## The Mechanics Mirror the Story

At some point Arthas loses his powers and *actually loses levels*. In an RPG, in 2002, the developers made your character regress.

I cannot stress how unusual this is. Every game tells you that progress is linear. You go up. You get stronger. Warcraft III looked at that convention and said: *what if, narratively, the character needs to feel diminished?* And then they just did it. Mechanically. In gameplay.

It's the kind of design decision that sounds insane in a meeting and then turns out to be the most memorable moment in the whole game.

---

## Every Mission Is Doing Something Different 

The mission design in Warcraft III is wildly varied and never gets stale. One mission you're playing MOBA-style with a handful of heroes. The next you're escaping your own capital alone as Arthas, chased by enemies with no base, no reinforcements, just vibes and a Frostmourne. Then suddenly you're managing three bases across two continents and someone has set the difficulty to "please stop."

The Night Elves' uproot mechanic, buildings that detach and walk immediately gave me SC2 flashbacks. As someone who spent most of their childhood watching Zerg creep across maps, seeing Blizzard play with that same idea of mobile, biological infrastructure was a little surreal. Honestly, the design DNA between the two games is everywhere once you start looking.

---

## The Outcasts, the Tragedy, and Why Illidan Deserved Better

The back half of the campaign introduces a quieter tragedy: Illidan, the naga, the blood elves. None of them are heroes. None of them are villains either. They're outcasts trying to survive in a world that's rejected them, scraping together alliances out of necessity, doing morally questionable things for understandable reasons.

And they lose.

Not to a great hero. Not in a triumphant final battle. They just... lose, because Arthas wins. And you helped him.

The final staircase scene, Arthas ascending alone, reflecting on every choice that brought him here is genuinely one of the most well-constructed moments I've seen in any medium. The game lets you walk in silence hearing the voices of those you wronged. You think about the purge. The plague. His father. The sword. All of it.

When he fuses with the Lich King at the top, the Scourge claims victory and the credits roll.

There is no catharsis. The villain wins. The heroes are scattered or dead or worse. And you sit there thinking: *I completed every mission. I won. Why does this feel terrible?*

---

## Final Verdict: A Story That Uses the Medium Properly

Warcraft III Reforged holds up. The story is genuinely excellent, not just "for a video game," but as a piece of narrative craft full stop. The pacing across multiple storylines is remarkably tight, the worldbuilding is woven into side quests and ambient dialogue rather than dumped in cutscenes, and the missions constantly find ways to reinforce the themes mechanically rather than just telling you about them.

But what really hit home were the characters. Despite the relatively short runtime, the game makes every major figure feel genuinely real. Each of them has their own goals, their own motivations, their own idea of what the right thing to do is — for themselves, for their people, for their race. Watching all of those threads play out simultaneously, and seeing how each character's choices ripple into everyone else's story, is something most big-budget films don't manage to pull off. 

But most of all: Arthas.

The descent into evil isn't something you watch from the outside. You are inside it, mission by mission, decision by decision. By the end you understand why he made every choice, even the catastrophic ones.

And that's the most unsettling, impressive thing Warcraft III does.

It makes you understand him.

It might even make you agree with him, in the moment.

And then you reach the top of the staircase, and you realize what that means.

---

*10/10. Would commit atrocities again.*
